Is Your AC Working Harder Than It Should Be?

[Author Name], Service Manager
[Month YYYY]

Every summer, we respond to hundreds of calls from homeowners whose air conditioners have stopped keeping up — not because the equipment failed, but because the system was never set up to succeed in the first place. Dirty coils, undersized ductwork, and neglected filters quietly steal efficiency until the system buckles under the season's first triple-digit week.

The good news? Most of these problems are entirely preventable with a little knowledge and a quick annual tune-up. In this issue, we're breaking down the top five causes of summertime AC failure and what you can do right now to keep your family comfortable all season long.

⚙️ 5 Ways to Maximize AC Efficiency This Summer
1

Change Your Filter Every 30–60 Days

A clogged filter forces your blower to work harder, increases energy consumption, and can cause the evaporator coil to freeze. Set a monthly reminder and use a MERV 8–11 filter for the best balance of airflow and filtration.

2

Set Your Thermostat to 78°F When Home

The U.S. Department of Energy recommends 78°F when home and 85–88°F when away. Every degree below 78°F increases your energy usage by roughly 3%. A smart thermostat can automate this and pay for itself in the first season.

3

Clear 2 Feet of Clearance Around Your Outdoor Unit

Bushes, mulch, and debris that crowd the condenser coil restrict airflow and cause the refrigerant to overheat. Keep the area clear and gently hose down the fins in early summer to remove dust and pollen buildup.

4

Seal Duct Leaks Before They Cost You

The U.S. EPA estimates that 20–30% of conditioned air is lost through leaky ducts in the average home. Sealing with mastic or foil tape — or having a professional perform a duct pressure test — can dramatically reduce utility bills.

5

Schedule a Tune-Up Before the Heat Hits

The best time to service your AC is before you need it most. A professional tune-up includes refrigerant charge verification, electrical connection tightening, coil cleaning, and a full system performance check — all of which extend equipment life and prevent costly mid-season failures.

Carbon Monoxide: The Silent Risk Every Heating Season Brings

[Author Name], Lead Technician
[Month YYYY]

Carbon monoxide poisoning sends more than 20,000 Americans to emergency rooms each year — and the vast majority of incidents happen in fall and winter when furnaces, boilers, and fireplaces are first fired up after months of inactivity. A cracked heat exchanger, a blocked flue, or a corroded gas valve can turn your heating system into a silent hazard.

The most important things you can do right now: test your CO detectors, replace any that are more than seven years old, and have a licensed technician inspect your furnace before the first cold snap. At Your HVAC Co., every furnace tune-up includes a heat exchanger inspection and combustion analysis — because your family's safety is not optional.

Why Spring Is the Best Time to Service Your AC (And Why Most People Wait Too Long)

[Author Name], Owner
[Month YYYY]

Every May, our phones start ringing with customers who turned on their AC for the first time since September — only to find it blowing warm air, making strange noises, or tripping the breaker. These aren't emergencies that happened overnight. They're the accumulated results of six months of sitting idle: refrigerant lines that shifted, contactors that corroded, and capacitors that weakened over winter.

Spring servicing isn't just about preventing breakdowns — it's about starting the season with a system that's ready to perform at its best. An AC that's properly tuned in March or April runs more efficiently, keeps your home more comfortable during peak heat, and typically lasts [3–5] years longer than one that goes season to season without attention.

🌱 Spring AC Startup — What Our Tune-Up Covers
1

Refrigerant Pressure Check & Leak Detection

Low refrigerant is the #1 cause of warm air on the first hot day. We check system pressures against manufacturer specifications and test for leaks at all connection points — not just add refrigerant and bill you for a bandage fix.

2

Condenser & Evaporator Coil Cleaning

Pollen, cottonwood, and debris coat condenser coils every spring in Arkansas. A dirty condenser coil can reduce efficiency by 20–30% and cause the compressor to overheat. We clean both coils to restore full heat transfer capacity.

3

Electrical Component Inspection

Capacitors, contactors, and wiring connections degrade over winter. We test starting and running capacitors, inspect contactors for pitting and burning, and tighten all electrical connections — preventing the most common summer failure points.

4

Blower Motor & Airflow Verification

We measure actual airflow against design specifications and check blower motor current draw, belt condition (if applicable), and bearing lubrication. Proper airflow is the foundation of comfort — and low airflow is a coil-freezing, efficiency-killing problem that's easy to miss.
