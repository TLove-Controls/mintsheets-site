⚡ Pro Tips — Room Airflow Design
  • Minimum ASHRAE 62.2 supply: 50 CFM per room regardless of load calculation.
  • For new construction, use 0.75 CFM/ft² as your starting point; adjust for window area and climate.
  • Register face velocity: aim for 500–750 FPM for supply registers to ensure adequate throw.
  • Bedrooms typically need 1–2 registers; living areas may need 2–4 for even distribution.
  • Check total CFM against 400 CFM/ton — deviation > 15% indicates a balancing or sizing issue.

How to Calculate Airflow Per Room (CFM)

The HVAC airflow per room calculator is designed for HVAC designers, installers, and field technicians who need to allocate the correct amount of conditioned airflow to each space in a building. Knowing the exact CFM requirement per room ensures balanced comfort, prevents hot/cold spots, and avoids over-pressurizing individual zones.

The most common simplified method uses a CFM per square foot factor — typically 0.75 CFM/ft² for standard residential spaces. For more precise results, you can input each room's sensible heat load in BTUH (calculated using the HVAC load calculator) and the tool derives CFM from the fundamental sensible heat equation: CFM = BTUH ÷ (1.08 × ΔT).

Sizing Supply Registers Per Room

Once CFM per room is known, the next step is selecting the right number and size of supply registers. Register face velocity should generally stay between 500 and 750 FPM for residential applications. Higher velocities increase throw but also increase noise; lower velocities reduce throw and can result in poor air mixing.

This calculator automatically suggests a register size based on standard register throat dimensions and a target face velocity. For the connecting ductwork, use the duct sizing calculator to determine the appropriate round or rectangular duct dimension for each branch run.

Balancing Total System Airflow

The system balance section verifies that the sum of all room CFM requirements aligns with the equipment's design airflow — typically 400 CFM per ton. A significant deviation indicates either an undersized system, an oversized system, or room load assignments that need adjustment. For static pressure verification across the duct system, use the static pressure calculator to confirm the system can deliver this airflow without restriction.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much CFM is needed per square foot?

For standard residential HVAC systems, a rule of thumb is 0.75 to 1.0 CFM per square foot. However, rooms with high solar gain (large west-facing windows) or high ceiling volume may require up to 1.25 CFM per square foot to maintain comfort.

What happens if a room has too little airflow?

Insufficient CFM results in "stagnant" air and temperature lagging. In cooling mode, the room will remain warm and humid; in heating mode, it will stay cold. Additionally, low airflow across the entire system can cause the evaporator coil to freeze.
